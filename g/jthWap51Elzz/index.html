Real Cottage Cheese

How to read this aisle: The tilt-and-drain test: tip the tub on its side. real cottage cheese releases a thin, watery whey that runs clear and fast; a gummed-up tub's liquid is thick, gluey and slow, clinging to the curd in ropes. The three-ingredient test (the one that never lies): read only the first three ingredients. You want cultured milk, cream and salt. If you see starch, maltodextrin, a gum, or a word you can't pronounce in those first three lines, put it back. The protein trick: if a tub screams a huge protein number on the front but lists whey protein concentrate high in the ingredients, that protein was boosted with powder, not built from real milk. Real curd doesn't need powder to be high in protein.

SKIP Breakstone's

Heritage brand (founded 1882) but tapioca starch comes right after milk and cream, doing the job cream should do; sodium runs high. The flavored 'cottage doubles' cups fall apart completely, stacking modified corn starch, added sugar, natural flavor and potassium sorbate (a preservative); under 12g protein. 'a dessert in a health costume.'

SKIP Corto

Breakstone's West Coast twin, same parent company (selling the heritage story since 1919). Low-fat tub lists modified food starch, calcium phosphate, xanthan gum and guar gum, plus added whey to prop up the protein number; sodium climbs to around 400mg per half-cup serving.

SKIP Hood

One of the most heavily engineered tubs found anywhere. A single low-fat container can carry whey protein concentrate, modified food starch and three separate gums (guar, locust bean and carrageenan), along with potassium sorbate and phosphoric acid; whipped and flavored versions pile on more. 'When a label needs three different gums just to hold itself together, the milk underneath was never rich enough.'

SKIP Great Value (Walmart store brand)

Cheap isn't the crime. what makes it cheap is. Stabilizer blend reads like a supplement bottle: maltodextrin, guar gum, carrageenan, mono- and diglycerides, and locust bean gum, finished with potassium sorbate. Fat-free version is busier and some tubs add polysorbate 80. The gums do the cream's job for pennies.

SKIP Prairie Farms

Markets hard on farm families, blue ribbons and World Dairy Expo awards, but the ingredient list has a stabilizer blend of five separate thickeners. guar gum, mono- and diglycerides, xanthan gum, carrageenan and carob bean gum. plus maltodextrin and potassium sorbate. 'The trophy on the front does not match the lab work in the back.'

SKIP Country Fresh (and regional value tubs following the same recipe)

Cultured nonfat milk dried out then rebuilt with maltodextrin, guar gum, carrageenan, mono- and diglycerides and locust bean gum, with natural and artificial flavor and a preservative. 'When the first real ingredient is fat-free milk and the next six lines are thickeners, you are buying engineering with a dairy logo on it.' Regional tubs rotate names region to region, so only the label is a reliable defense.

REAL Daisy

Entire ingredient list is three words. cultured skim milk, cream, salt. No starch, no gums, no preservatives. Delivers about 13g protein and usually costs about $3.50, often the cheapest tub on the shelf. 'The most honest cottage cheese in the store is also one of the cheapest.'

REAL Good Culture

Label openly promises no gums, no carrageenan, no preservatives, and the ingredients back it up: milk, cream, sea salt, and live and active cultures. About $5.60, but you're paying for real dairy and probiotics; about 14g protein. Founder built it after he couldn't find an additive-free cottage cheese; the brand chefs and recipe developers reach for.

REAL Friendship Dairies

Making cottage cheese for more than 100 years without ruining it: cultured nonfat milk, milk, salt and enzymes. no gums, no starch. Some tubs reach 16g protein the old-fashioned way with real dairy, and it's usually cheaper than the boutique options.

Real Mozzarella

How to read this aisle: The two words that tell you you're being shorted are 'cellulose' and 'imitation.' The honest move on shredded cheese isn't a pre-shredded bag. buy the block and shred it yourself (avoids the anti-caking coating and the higher contamination risk of pre-cut cheese). Read the ingredient list, not the big print. a '100%' or 'real cheese' claim on the front doesn't override added wood fiber and starch on the back. SKIP Great Value Shredded Mozzarella (Walmart store brand)

After the cheese itself, lists potato starch and powdered cellulose (the polite name for wood pulp) added to prevent caking, plus natamycin (a mold-inhibiting fungicide). It was Walmart's Great Value grated cheese caught in the 2016 lab testing with cellulose levels far past what the industry considers acceptable. The coating is why store-brand shreds turn greasy and clumpy when melted.

SKIP Sargento

Markets itself as '100% real natural cheese,' and the cheese is real, but the shredded mozzarella still carries potato starch and natamycin coating every shred. the same anti-caking treatment as the cheaper bags. In 2024 Sargento recalled a batch of shredded cheese over possible listeria contamination (that batch went to food service, not branded bags), a reminder that pre-shredded, surface-treated cheese carries more risk than a sealed block.

SKIP Kraft

Shredded mozzarella carries the same lineup. potato starch, cellulose powder, calcium sulfate and natamycin. Earned its spot because of the receipt: in 2016 Kraft Heinz was named in class-action lawsuits over its '100% grated parmesan,' which independent testing pegged at around 3.8% cellulose. Kraft's defense was that the wood pulp was within legal limits. 'which is true and also exactly the problem.' The '100%' claim can't be backed up by the ingredients.

SKIP String cheese (national brands or store versions)

Low-moisture part-skim mozzarella that's been heated and pulled into a peelable rope then individually wrapped. It started as real mozzarella, but by the time it's processed for peel, shelf life and a kid's backpack, it's a snack product more than a cheese. often part-skim and rubbery, built to survive a lunch box rather than to taste like much.

SKIP Imitation mozzarella (on cheap frozen pizzas)

Often labeled 'imitation mozzarella' right on the box. Contains water, vegetable oil, potato and modified starch, a milk protein called rennet casein, gelatin, and titanium dioxide (a white pigment added to make oil and starch look like cheese). Not cheese at all. vegetable oil, starch and a chemical whitener engineered to stretch and melt like the real thing.

Real Shredded Cheese

How to read this aisle: The water-dissolve test: drop a handful of shredded cheese into warm water and stir 30 seconds. Real cheese softens, clumps together and releases visible milk fat; filler-heavy cheese leaves a cloudy, starchy residue and the shreds resist breaking down because the cellulose/starch coating acts as a waterproof barrier. Milky-white water with grit at the bottom is the filler separating, not cheese dissolving. The bag-shake test: shake the bag hard for 5 seconds and look at the bottom. A light dusting of powder is normal; a thick, visible layer of white powder means heavy cellulose and starch coating you're paying for by weight. Filler names to watch for: cellulose (powdered wood pulp), potato starch, corn starch, natamycin (synthetic mold inhibitor). The same filler playbook runs across every price point from budget to premium. only the marketing changes.

SKIP Kraft

Bags say 'natural cheese' on the front in bold, but the ingredient list includes cellulose powder, potato starch, corn starch, calcium sulfate and natamycin. a triple filler system plus a synthetic mold inhibitor on a product marketed as natural. Kraft's parent has faced class-action scrutiny over the word 'natural' on products containing processed wood fiber and synthetic preservatives.

SKIP Great Value (Walmart store brand)

Ingredient list includes cellulose, potato starch, corn starch and natamycin. Priced as the budget option, but the filler-to-cheese ratio means less real cheese per ounce than the label suggests. The thick layer of white powder at the bottom of the bag is the anti-caking cocktail you're paying for by weight.

SKIP Borden

Heritage dairy name (over 160 years) but heritage doesn't equal quality. contains cellulose, potato starch and natamycin, the same industrial filler recipe as every budget brand. Borden filed for bankruptcy in 2020 citing declining milk prices and crushing debt; under that financial pressure every product line gets optimized for margin.

SKIP Crystal Farms

Markets itself as mid-tier with packaging implying farm freshness; ingredient list includes potato starch, powdered cellulose and natamycin. Home melt tests reveal a gritty, separated texture. a hallmark of excessive starch coating; the shreds don't melt smoothly because they're cheese wrapped in a layer of industrial powder.

SKIP Kroger brand

Private-label ingredient list includes cellulose, potato starch, corn starch and natamycin. the exact same filler profile as Kraft and Great Value, repackaged under a store brand that implies freshness and local sourcing. 'There is nothing local about powdered cellulose.'

SKIP Sargento

Built its entire brand identity around being real, natural cheese and charges a premium for it, but flip the bag: potato starch, powdered cellulose, natamycin. the exact same three fillers found in the budget brands it markets against. 'Arguably the widest marketing-to-reality gap on the entire shelf.' You're paying for a brand story, not a better product.

REAL Tillamook

Farmer-owned cooperative (founded 1909, Oregon coast). Uses real aged cheese with only potato starch as an anti-caking agent. no cellulose, no corn starch, no natamycin. Remarkably short ingredient list; cooperative ownership means farmers control the product. About $1–$2 more per bag than Kraft. for a product that is actually what it claims to be, 'that is not a premium, that is a bargain.'

REAL Cabot

Cooperative of New England dairy farm families (founded 1919). Shredded cheese contains potato starch only for anti-caking in most varieties. no cellulose, no natamycin, no corn starch. Sits in the same price range as Sargento but with an ingredient list that actually delivers on the promise Sargento only markets.

REAL Organic Valley

Cooperative of organic farmers (founded 1988). USDA certified organic, organic cheese as the primary ingredient, only potato starch for anti-caking. no cellulose, no natamycin, no corn starch, no modified food starch, no natural flavors. 'The cleanest shredded cheese widely available.' Most expensive (about $2–$3 more per bag than Kraft) but you're paying for cheese, not wood pulp and starch. the gold standard on the shelf.

Where To Buy Butter

How to read this aisle: Butter has a legal definition (21 USC 321a, 21 CFR 101.67): to be called butter it must contain at least 80% milk fat. that's the entire bar. There's no separate tier for supermarket vs premium vs gourmet, so once a product clears 80% it's legally the same product regardless of price or packaging. The price you pay has almost nothing to do with what's in the package and almost everything to do with which door you walked through. Before your next run, check your regular store's per-pound butter price against Aldi's. if the gap is more than a dollar or two, you're paying for the name on the door, not quality. The three recommended stores all sell grade AA butter well above the 80% milk-fat floor from cows never given rBST, for roughly $3–$4/lb consistently without games.

SKIP Walmart (Great Value salted butter)

Mildest offender. cheapest on sticker price (about $2.42 for an 8-oz pack, under $5/lb) and not overcharging you. But in a structured side-by-side taste test against other store brands, testers flagged it specifically for being oversalted to the point the salt overwhelmed the actual dairy flavor. Still earns grade AA, but 'acceptable range' isn't 'well-balanced.' You get exactly what you paid for and not a cent more in quality.

SKIP Winn-Dixie

A straight receipt: a multi-reporter survey priced an identical Thanksgiving-staples basket at 19 stores; Walmart rang it up at $76.65, Winn-Dixie at $105.67. a 38% markup for the exact same items, same week, same market, with butter one of the line items driving the gap. Not marketed as a discount chain, but shoppers who treat it as a normal-priced everyday store are paying real money basket after basket.

SKIP Publix

Highest basket total of the three compared ($109.62, even above Winn-Dixie). A separate area survey found Publix charging $5.26–$5.79/lb for standard sweet cream butter versus $3.69/lb at Aldi in the same market. roughly 40–50% more for a legally identical product. Its GreenWise organic line is genuinely well-reviewed and competes honestly at $9.59/lb, but the everyday plain sweet-cream stick most people grab carries the 40–50% premium with nothing on the label to justify it.

SKIP Whole Foods ('whole paycheck')

A documented, repeatable pricing pattern. In the same multi-store comparison, a dozen large eggs cost $1.39 at Target but $4.19 at Whole Foods. three times the price, same product category, same week and region. That gap shows up as a consistent pattern across the whole dairy case, butter included. When the markup is this consistent across basic staples, 'premium sourcing starts to look less like a principled stance and more like a pricing strategy with a values-based paint job.'

SKIP The Fresh Market

The biggest number on the list: charged $9.59 for a single pound of plain butter. the highest recorded anywhere in the comparison. Full 18-item basket rang up at $74.82 versus $37.55 for the identical items at Aldi. essentially double. For a plain stick of butter with no organic certification, no imported origin story, nothing distinguishing it on the label, that $9.59 tag is 'the single most expensive way to buy a legally identical product anywhere in this lineup.'

REAL Aldi (Countryside Creamery)

Aldi's in-house butter, $3.23–$4.09/lb depending on week and location, dropping as low as $1.99/lb during Thanksgiving/Christmas seasonal sales. undercuts nearly every other store. Grade AA, cows certified rBST-free. In blind side-by-side comparisons against Kerrygold (which costs 2–3x more), plenty of tasters genuinely couldn't tell the Irish-style Countryside Creamery apart from the real thing. Butter freezes for up to a year, so stocking up on the $1.99 sale can cut annual butter spending by half or more.

REAL Costco (Kirkland Signature)

Sells in 4-lb bulk packs for around $11.79–$12.99, which works out to roughly $2.95–$3.25/lb. in line with everyday prices, sometimes cheaper. Same grade AA standard, same legal butter classification, just sold at scale with warehouse-club margins. The only catch is committing to 4 lbs at once, which freezer storage solves. 'One of the best per-pound deals in the entire grocery landscape.'

REAL Trader Joe's

$3.99/lb. not the single cheapest (Aldi undercuts it) but offers consistency: no weekly sales to chase, no markup games. Grade A cream from cows never given rBST hormone, priced the same every time whether a random Tuesday or the week before a holiday. Reviewers call it solid, dependable, supermarket-quality butter; also stocks well-regarded imported options (a French cultured butter, sometimes a New Zealand import) for the premium tier without premium-grocer prices.

Real Beef Broth

How to read this aisle: The words that tell you you're holding flavor-water: yeast extract, hydrolyzed protein, natural flavoring, and caramel color (yeast extract and hydrolyzed protein are forms of free glutamate. MSG by other names). Read the ingredient list in order: if beef and bones come first and the list is short, you've got real stock. If yeast extract, hydrolyzed protein or caramel color is carrying the load, put it back. Real stock gels. chill it and it turns to jelly from the gelatin in the bones. That's the sign it's the real thing; a carton that stays liquid doesn't have enough real bone in it. Real beef stock is nearly free to make at home: ask the butcher for beef bones, roast them brown, and simmer with an onion, carrot and celery for a few hours.

SKIP Pacific Foods Organic Beef Broth

The cleanest of the skips and 'fine in a pinch,' but the organic seal makes you stop reading the rest of the label. Ingredients: organic beef stock, then organic beef flavor, sea salt, organic yeast extract and organic caramel color. Making the yeast extract and caramel color organic doesn't make them not yeast extract and caramel color. same flavored-and-tinted formula as the conventional stuff, only grown organically, and you're paying an organic premium for a broth still leaning on glutamate for taste and coloring for looks.

SKIP College Inn Beef Broth

Around since the 1920s; the carton says heritage but the ingredient list is a chemistry set. After the broth: hydrolyzed protein from soy, corn and wheat, then monosodium glutamate (actual MSG spelled out), plus caramel color, autolyzed yeast extract, and two more flavor enhancers. disodium inosinate and guanylate. A whole stack of glutamates layered to fake a richness of beef flavor that isn't there; barely any real beef doing the work.

SKIP Store brands (Great Value and generic supermarket labels)

A dollar or two and it shows. Water at the top, then things like modified palm oil, maltodextrin, autolyzed yeast extract, a little beef extract, disodium guanylate and caramel color. The beef is so far down the list 'it's almost a rumor'. salted, tinted water with a whisper of beef.

SKIP Swanson Beef Broth (owned by Campbell's)

Front proudly says 'no MSG added,' but read the asterisk: the footnote admits glutamate occurs naturally in the yeast extract they added. the whole trick. Ingredients: beef stock, salt, natural flavoring, yeast extract, a little mirepoix and cane sugar. They didn't add MSG, they added yeast extract, which delivers the same free glutamate, then advertised 'no MSG.' Better than a bouillon cube but still thin, salty, sweet broth propped up with glutamate. and it won't gel because there's not enough real bone.

SKIP Better Than Bouillon Beef Base

America's Test Kitchen named it their winner and it makes a savory cup fast. but look at why it wins: roasted beef with concentrated beef stock, then salt, hydrolyzed soy protein, sugar, corn syrup solids, flavoring, yeast extract and caramel color. It tastes strong because it's loaded with salt and glutamate, not because it's loaded with beef; the testers who picked it noted it had little actual beef and wished it had more. 'The best the category does, and it's still a salt-and-glutamate paste with a little beef'. concentrated flavoring, not concentrated stock.

SKIP Beef bouillon cubes (Wyler's, Knorr, Herb-Ox)

Where beef broth bottoms out. Ingredient list is almost a parody: salt first, then hydrolyzed soy protein, sugar, MSG, beef fat, palm oil, maltodextrin, caramel color, and beef stock nearly last. One cube can carry 900–960mg of sodium (close to half a day's worth) and as many as nine different forms of glutamate. The caramel coloring carries a compound (4-MEI) that California warns about and Europe caps far more tightly. 'A bouillon costume.'

REAL Kitchen Basics Unsalted Beef Stock (made by McCormick)

The cleanest mainstream carton and the best you can grab at a regular grocery store. Starts with real beef stock slow-simmered with vegetables and herbs, no MSG, no caramel color, no added salt, even certified heart-healthy. It still uses a little yeast extract and natural flavor so it's not as pure as homemade, but it's real stock first and flavoring last. the order you want. and the unsalted version lets you control the salt.

REAL Kettle & Fire Beef Bone Broth

Real stock that behaves like real stock. pour it cold and it's thick and jiggly because there's genuine collagen in it. Slow-simmers bones from grass-fed, pasture-raised cattle for 20+ hours with no preservatives and no glutamate tricks, then seals it shelf-stable. Just beef, bones, vegetables and thyme. the actual recipe; the gel in the fridge is the proof. In thousands of stores (Target to Walmart); costs more than a Swanson carton because it's mostly beef and bones instead of water and yeast extract.

REAL Bonafide Provisions Beef Bone Broth

The gold standard for a clean gelling stock. Started by nutritionist mom Sharon Brown. Organic, made from grass-fed bones simmered 18+ hours with a splash of apple cider vinegar to pull the minerals out, sold frozen so it needs no preservatives. Sets into a thick, unmistakable gel. the clearest sign on the list that you're holding real stock and not flavored water. Priciest of the three; found in the freezer at Whole Foods and Sprouts. 'The closest thing to the pot your grandmother would have simmered all day.'

Real Vegetable Broth

How to read this aisle: Turn the green carton over first. If you see yeast extract, hydrolyzed protein, or a 'no MSG' badge working overtime, put it back. Yeast extract and hydrolyzed protein are loaded with free glutamic acid. the same flavor-punch molecule found in MSG. used to make thin, watery liquid taste slow-simmered. Under FDA section 403, calling a product 'no MSG' is misleading when it's full of free-glutamic ingredients like these. Disodium inosinate and disodium guanylate are flavor potentiators that amplify glutamate. their presence signals there's glutamate in the product to amplify, label 'MSG' or not. Real vegetable stock is almost free: save onion skins, carrot peels, celery ends and leek tops, simmer with water and a little salt for 45 minutes.

SKIP Kitchen Basics (McCormick-owned)

The cleanest costume. sells itself as restaurant-quality ('less sodium, no added MSG, real ingredients') but after the stock and sea salt come cane sugar, yeast extract, and natural flavor. Yeast extract is the free-glutamate shortcut sitting right under a 'no MSG' banner, and 'natural flavor' is its own black box. The least offensive brand (no palm oil, no caramel color) but asks $4–$5 a carton for the impression of honesty; 'a fancier costume is still a costume.'

SKIP Great Value (Walmart house broth)

Lists disodium inosinate and disodium guanylate. flavor potentiators that only work synergistically with glutamate to amplify a savory hit, so their presence means there's glutamate in the product whether or not 'MSG' appears on the label. Alongside them the regular version lists yeast extract, sugar and dextrose (more sugar) and natural flavoring. Retails around $1.50. 'salted water with a chemistry set stirred in.'

SKIP Swanson (owned by Campbell's)

Where the 'no MSG' game went to court. In 2023 a shopper sued Campbell's over the front-of-carton 'no MSG added' claim; a judge sided with Campbell's because a tiny disclaimer admitted 'a small amount of glutamate occurs naturally in yeast extract'. i.e. the company admitted the glutamate is in there, just in fine print. Second and third ingredients are salt and cane sugar (sugar in vegetable broth), then yeast extract and natural flavoring; sodium runs around 800mg a cup (more than a third of a day's).

SKIP College Inn (owned by Del Monte)

Didn't just get accused of the 'no MSG' trick. got named in a class-action lawsuit over it (a wave of California suits against broth and noodle makers). Turn the carton over and you'll find yeast extract and natural flavors, the very ingredients that under the FDA's own rule make a 'no MSG' claim misleading, while College Inn makes 'no MSG' a selling point. 'A handshake from someone whose other hand is in your pocket.'

SKIP Better Than Bouillon

Real pureed vegetables on top, but two spots down is the tell: hydrolyzed soy protein (the other big free-glutamate ingredient, named by the FDA right next to yeast extract). And it doesn't stop there. also gives you yeast extract, plus sugar, plus maltodextrin, plus natural flavors: two separate sources of manufactured glutamate stacked into one product. Frustrating because they had real vegetables and could have stopped there.

SKIP Knorr (owned by Unilever)

The worst offender. Knorr Homestyle Vegetable Stock tub's first oil is palm oil, followed by hydrolyzed yeast extract, maltodextrin, caramel color and sulfur dioxide. 'a science experiment with a vegetable photo on the lid.' The bouillon cubes go further, listing actual monosodium glutamate, plus hydrogenated palm oil, disodium inosinate, a petroleum-derived preservative called TBHQ, and caramel coloring to fake a brown a broth never had. (In 2017 Europe set the first hard ceiling on added glutamate; in America there is no limit at all.)

REAL Kettle & Fire

Started 2015 by brothers Justin and Nick Mares who couldn't find a clean organic broth. The whole vegetable-carton label is water, organic tomato paste, sea salt, and concentrated organic carrots, onions, leeks and mushrooms. that's the entire list. No yeast extract, no natural flavor hiding anything. Certified organic, non-GMO verified, Environmental Working Group flagged zero ingredient concerns; around $4–$5 at most chains.

REAL Brodo

From James Beard Award-winning chef Marco Canora, who revived his health with broth and started selling it out of a window in his East Village restaurant in 2014. Vegan broth built from organic vegetables, mushrooms and wild seaweed for the savory background. no concentrates, no extracts, no manufactured flavor of any kind. You can read every word without a chemistry degree; the catch is price and availability (more of a specialty/online buy, not cheap).

REAL Bonafide Provisions

The #1 pick. 'the one I'd put in your cart today.' From nutritionist Sharon Brown (who healed her son's gut with homemade broth) and her chef husband. Label: filtered water, onions, celery, carrots, sea salt, mushrooms, leeks, collard greens, spinach, broccoli, fennel, tomatoes, parsley, peppercorns, garlic. 12 real vegetables and that's the whole thing. No yeast extract, no natural flavor, no sugar, no oil; there's even a no-salt-added version. 'It tastes like soup because it is soup.'

Real Sandwich Bread

How to read this aisle: The gold 'whole grain' stamp is a private industry seal (Whole Grains Council, created 2005), not a government guarantee. Its most common 'basic' version only requires 8g of whole grain per serving with NO upper limit on how much refined flour fills out the rest of the slice. The 100% stamp requires every grain be whole (min 16g); the 50%+ stamp requires at least half be whole (8g floor). 'Multigrain' is not regulated at all. it only means more than one grain is present and says nothing about whether any are whole. The 10-second fiber check: flip to the nutrition-facts panel and read grams of dietary fiber per slice. Real whole-grain bread carries roughly 2–3g fiber per slice; bread that's mostly refined flour (even with a whole-grain or multigrain claim) usually shows only about 1g. When the stamp and the fiber number disagree, the fiber number is the truth. fiber can't be conditioned or colored the way a crust or a name can. If it's 1g on a 'whole grain' bread, put it back.

SKIP Martin's Famous Potato Bread

The golden color isn't from butter or eggs the way the branding implies. the bag lists turmeric color and annatto color as ingredients doing the visual work a richer dough would do. First ingredient is unbleached enriched wheat flour (plain refined flour) and there's no whole-grain claim anywhere. 'Nothing dishonest once you read it, but the golden glow is doing a lot more emotional work than the flour inside.' About $3.99 for an 18-oz loaf.

SKIP Pepperidge Farm Farmhouse Hearty White

Marketed as 'the bread you'd bake yourself... crafted with premium ingredients over 75 years,' but the ingredients read enriched wheat flour, water, sugar, yeast, then monoglycerides, DATEM, and calcium propionate and sorbic acid to extend freshness. standard commercial dough chemistry, the same category found on far cheaper loaves. No whole grain at all and no claim of any. About $4.99 for 24 oz. roughly $259/year on the word 'homemade' over an ordinary commercially-conditioned ingredient list.

SKIP Country Hearth Multigrain (Pan-O-Gold Baking Co.)

Name and marketing ('whole grain goodness') suggest a grain-forward loaf, but the first ingredient is enriched unbleached flour (refined), with the whole-grain mix not appearing until the third ingredient after water. 'Multigrain' only means more than one grain is present. it doesn't mean the grains are whole and carries no minimum requirement. Visible grains/seeds baked onto the crust do the persuading; the crumb underneath is mostly refined flour. About $4.12 for 20 oz.

SKIP Aunt Millie's Live Light Whole Grain

A 40-calorie-a-slice diet bread. To hit that low calorie count the recipe leans on polydextrose, resistant wheat starch and wheat protein isolate. engineered fillers that add bulk and fiber without adding calories or real grain. After all that engineering the loaf carries just 9g whole grain per serving, only 1g above the Whole Grain Council's 8g basic-stamp minimum. Polydextrose counts as fiber on the label but behaves nothing like the bran in a real whole-grain kernel. About $4.29 for 20 oz. 'engineered to a calorie target first and a grain content second.'

SKIP Brownberry Oatnut Whole Grain (18-oz, under Bimbo Bakeries)

Carries the same gold whole-grains branding as the rest of the lineup, but this specific Oatnut loaf has only 9g whole grain per serving. while Brownberry's multi-whole-grain and 12 Grains & Seeds breads under the identical gold stamp carry 18–19g, roughly double. Same company, same logo, same shelf, same stamp family, but the actual whole-grain content swings by a factor of two depending on which bag you grab, with no way to tell without doing the math on the back. About $4.49.

SKIP Cheesecake Factory brown bread (made under license by Bimbo Bakehouse)

The one that ended up in federal court. A 2022 class action (Illinois) alleges the bread's dark color comes from caramel coloring and dried molasses, not from bran or whole grain, and that visible pieces of grain are placed on top deliberately to reinforce a 'healthy' impression. Per the complaint, enriched wheat flour is the most predominant ingredient with whole wheat flour showing up third. A judge dismissed several claims (ruling color/appearance alone weren't a reasonable basis), but the case never disputed the ingredient order or coloring method. About $4.99.

REAL Vermont Bread Company

Started 1978 by Lisa Lorimer in an 800-sq-ft barn. Ingredient list: organic wheat flour, organic whole wheat flour, organic rolled oats, organic cane sugar, organic molasses, with almost everything else under 2%. No high-fructose corn syrup, no artificial preservatives, sourced entirely from certified organic farms. Real Hot Dogs

How to read this aisle: If 'mechanically separated' anything is your hot dog's first ingredient, you're not eating a cut of meat. you're eating a bone-pressed byproduct with flavoring added back. Federal rule (9 CFR 319.180) allows mechanically separated poultry to be up to 15% of the recipe. The press/simmer test: simmer two dogs 5 minutes. A hot dog loaded with mechanically separated meat and added water tends to leave the water cloudy, sometimes with a thin white foam. Then press each between two fingers. real whole-muscle meat holds a firm shape and springs back; a paste-based product feels soft and mushy. Processed-meat context: in 2015 the WHO's cancer research arm classified processed meat (hot dogs by name) as a Group 1 carcinogen. same strength of evidence category as tobacco (not the same danger level); every ~50g/day (about one hot dog) is linked to about an 18% higher colorectal cancer risk. One move: flip the package over. if the first ingredient starts with 'mechanically separated,' switch brands next trip.

SKIP Ball Park

Mechanically separated chicken is ingredient number one, pork is second. That paste-like poultry product (USDA's own paperwork calls it 'paste-like'. bones forced through a sieve under high pressure) isn't a minor add-in here, it's the base of the entire hot dog. No recall attached; the issue is sitting in plain sight on every package and most shoppers never flip it over.

Real Chicken

How to read this aisle: Real chicken is one ingredient. chicken. with no injected solution and no water soaked in. Turn the package over before it goes in the cart; the front is a sales pitch, the back is the truth. Check 1. the solution statement near the weight: if it says 'enhanced,' 'marinated,' 'contains up to 15% solution,' or 'X% retained water,' you're holding a plumped bird paying for salt water. Real chicken says 'no solution added,' or better yet 'air chilled.' Check 2. the hormone line: if the front brags 'no hormones,' the small print has an asterisk admitting that's true of all chicken. That asterisk is the tell that the front of the pack is selling you nothing. Raw chicken is legally allowed to carry salmonella (the government doesn't classify it as an illegal contaminant), which is why a brand tied to an outbreak can stay on the shelf.

SKIP Enhanced / plumped chicken (a practice across value shelves and store brands)

Not one brand but a practice: chicken injected with a solution of water, salt and sodium phosphates (often labeled 'enhanced' or 'contains up to 15% chicken broth'). Adds weight you pay for by the pound plus a few hundred extra milligrams of sodium you never asked for; the water cooks right back out. Advocates estimate Americans hand over around $2 billion a year for the added weight.

SKIP '100% Natural' / 'No Hormones' brands (e.g. Sanderson Farms)

'Natural' on chicken is only a processing word. it means no artificial ingredients were added after slaughter and says nothing about antibiotics, how the bird lived, or quality. And every chicken in America is hormone-free by law, so a brand bragging 'no hormones' is selling a feature every competitor has too. 'The label is marketing wallpaper'. you pay a premium for a green-sounding label doing all the work in your head.

SKIP Perdue

The friendly-farmer ads and 'humanely raised' on the pack. Animal-welfare advocates challenged the 'humanely raised' labeling and the company walked some of those claims back. On a separate front, Perdue agreed to pay $14.75 million to settle claims it conspired with other producers to hold down what chicken farmers were paid. 'Conventional industrial chicken with a warmer story on the front than the back can support.'

SKIP Foster Farms

From 2013 into 2014, Foster Farms chicken was tied to a salmonella outbreak that sickened 634 people across 29 states and Puerto Rico; 38% were hospitalized (about double the usual rate) and the strains were antibiotic-resistant. For roughly a year the company issued no recall. because salmonella on raw chicken is legal. Federal inspectors temporarily shut a California plant over filthy conditions before the company finally pulled some product. (Context: the EU bans the chlorine carcass rinse American plants use; the US does not.)

SKIP Tyson

In July 2021 Tyson recalled roughly 8.9 million pounds of ready-to-eat chicken (strips, diced, fully cooked) shipped to stores, schools, hospitals and nursing homes over listeria. the outbreak was linked to one death and additional illnesses. Tyson also sat at the center of the chicken price-fixing scandal, turning cooperator for the DOJ's investigation into producers rigging chicken prices and paying civil settlements. Industrial chicken from the largest operation in the country, so 'everything that goes wrong goes wrong at 9-million-pound scale.'

SKIP Pilgrim's Pride

The #1 skip 'because it hit your wallet on purpose.' In 2021 Pilgrim's Pride pleaded guilty in federal court to a criminal charge of price-fixing. conspiring to suppress competition and rig bids on broiler chicken. and paid a criminal fine of $107 million. The scheme ran for years and touched the price of chicken sold to grocers and restaurants nationwide. 'They didn't contaminate your chicken, they picked your pocket.'

REAL Kirkland Signature Organic (Costco)

The organic seal does real work: USDA organic chicken raised without antibiotics, on organic feed, with outdoor access, and no injected salt-water solution padding the weight. Proof you can verify: USDA organic seal, an ingredient line that reads 'chicken,' and no percent-solution statement anywhere. 'The cleanest chicken in the building at a warehouse price, not a boutique one.'

REAL Mary's (Pitman Family Farms)

California Central Valley; started 1954 by Don Pitman, birds named after Rick Pitman's wife Mary (who spent 25 years reading labels after additives made her sick), now run by the third generation. Air-chills the birds instead of soaking in chlorinated water (so no absorbed water padding weight) and raises without antibiotics. In 2013 became the first poultry producer verified by the Non-GMO Project. Proof on the package: air-chilled, Non-GMO Project verified, welfare certifications you can look up. Natural-food stores and West Coast grocers, a dollar or two more.

REAL Bell & Evans

The #1 real pick. Oldest branded chicken company in America (roots to 1894, Pennsylvania). In 1998 became the first major US producer to raise its birds with no antibiotics ever. years before the rest of the industry. Since 2005 air-chills 100% of its birds with cold air, never a chlorinated water bath (water chilling can leave a bird carrying up to 12% of its weight in absorbed water; air chilling adds none). Cleanest label in the case: 'no antibiotics ever, 100% air-chilled, one ingredient. chicken.' Whole Foods and a growing list of grocers; 'the bird a lot of chefs quietly buy for themselves.'

Real Mayonnaise

How to read this aisle: Real mayonnaise is legally at least 65% oil plus egg and vinegar with no starches or gums allowed; egg yolk is the only emulsifier allowed in true mayo. Turn the jar over and read it: real mayo is oil, egg, acid and salt with the good oil listed first and no starch or gums. If you see high fructose corn syrup, modified starch, the word 'dressing,' or olive oil buried fourth, put it back. 'Real' on the label is the legal floor, not the goal. real and clean are two different words.

SKIP Hellmann's (Best Foods out west)

Labeled 'real' and does meet the legal definition of mayonnaise with egg as the only emulsifier, but the ingredient list is soybean oil, water, eggs, vinegar, salt, sugar, lemon juice, calcium disodium EDTA and natural flavors. added sugar you don't need, a chelating preservative (EDTA), vague natural flavors, and a base of genetically modified soybean oil. The cheapest version of mayo dressed up with the word 'real.'

SKIP Kraft mayo and store brands like Great Value

Same idea as Hellmann's, fewer dollars, usually a longer additive list: soybean oil, water, eggs, sugar, vinegar, EDTA and natural flavors, sometimes with extra modified starch to firm it up cheaply. Built to a price. lowest cost oil, a touch of sugar, preservatives to keep it shelf-stable for a year.

SKIP Olive oil and avocado oil mayonnaise from big brands like Hellmann's and Kraft

A health halo it didn't earn. Soybean or canola oil is almost always the first ingredient and the olive or avocado oil you paid extra for shows up fourth or fifth in a small splash. Marketed as olive oil mayo but mostly the same cheap oil as the regular jar.

SKIP Light and reduced-fat mayonnaise

The diet trap. Pulling the oil out means replacing it with water, modified food starch, sugar and gums. Once it drops below the 65% oil line it legally can't be called mayonnaise (becomes a dressing). Trading real oil for starch and sugar and a gluey texture. mostly thickened water with a memory of mayonnaise.

SKIP Egg-free vegan mayo (Just Mayo and Vegenaise style)

Not mayonnaise by definition. With no egg, they rebuild the structure with pea or potato protein, modified starch and thickening gums, usually with some oil and a syrup for sweetness. Just Mayo got a 2015 FDA warning for calling its egg-free product mayo. Only egg-free and usually more processed, not healthier than regular mayo.

SKIP Miracle Whip

The worst. an impostor that built an empire on not being mayonnaise. Ingredients: water, soybean oil, high fructose corn syrup, vinegar, modified corn starch, eggs, salt, natural flavor and potassium sorbate. Far less oil than real mayo, with corn syrup and starch where the oil should be, so the government won't let it use the word mayonnaise. It's a sweetened, starchy, watered-down dressing sold at the same price as the real thing.

REAL Sir Kensington's avocado oil mayonnaise

The accessible clean one. Uses avocado oil as its only oil. no soybean, no canola. along with cage-free eggs, vinegar and lemon, with no EDTA and no added sugar in that version. A short, honest label. Found at most grocery stores and Target; costs more because avocado oil and cage-free eggs cost more.

REAL Primal Kitchen avocado oil mayo

The clean champion of the category, created by Mark Sisson. Whole list is avocado oil, organic vinegar, organic eggs and egg yolks, salt and rosemary extract. No soybean or canola oil, no added sugar, no EDTA, no natural flavors, and it comes in a glass jar. Best-selling natural mayo in the country; ingredient-for-ingredient about as close to homemade as a store jar gets.

REAL Homemade mayonnaise (with Duke's and Chosen Foods as jarred backups)

The best. costs almost nothing and takes 5 minutes: one egg yolk, about a cup of a good oil like avocado or light olive oil, a teaspoon of lemon juice or vinegar, a pinch of salt. Zero sugar or preservative because you didn't add any. If buying a jar, Duke's is the cleanest old-school brand (the one major mayo with no sugar) and Chosen Foods is another solid avocado option.

Real Lime Juice

How to read this aisle: Real lime juice is one ingredient. the juice of a lime. The warning words that tell you you're being shorted are 'from concentrate,' 'high fructose corn syrup,' and any dye with a number after it. Turn the bottle over: if the first ingredient is lime juice and the list is short, you found the real thing. Two tablespoons of good bottled juice equals about one fresh lime.

SKIP Store brands (Great Value, Kroger, etc.)

The cheapest bottle; a dollar or two and technically contains lime. Almost always water and lime juice from concentrate propped up with a preservative like sodium benzoate or sulfates. Reconstituted, boiled down far away, shipped and watered back up, so the flavor is flat and slightly bitter with none of the brightness of a fresh lime.

SKIP ReaLime

The most honest-sounding name but the bottle says otherwise: water, lime juice from concentrate, lime oil, and then two preservatives. sodium benzoate and sodium metabisulfite. Reconstituted concentrate with a sulfate and a benzoate added. Sodium benzoate mixed with citrus acid can form tiny amounts of benzene, a known carcinogen (the FDA's 2006 soft-drink investigation).

SKIP Nellie & Joe's Famous Key West Lime Juice

Sounds like a family operation in the Florida Keys, but the ingredients are filtered water, Key West lime juice from concentrate, and sodium benzoate as a preservative. the same reconstituted-concentrate-plus-benzoate formula as the cheaper bottles, only with a charming name and a higher price. Standard for key lime pie because it's tart and consistent, not because it's fresh.

SKIP Sweetened lime mixers (Master of Mixes and margarita mix bottles)

The bartender's shortcut; sit in the lime juice lane but are a sugar bomb. high fructose corn syrup or sugar near the top, citric acid for fake tartness, natural and artificial flavors, often a yellow dye. Barely any actual lime; the sweetener and flavoring carry it. If it's sweet before you add anything, it was never really lime juice.

SKIP Rose's sweetened lime juice

The worst. the green bottle behind a million bar wells. Ingredients in order: water, high fructose corn syrup, lime juice concentrate, sodium metabisulfite, natural flavors, yellow 5 and blue 1. Corn syrup is the second ingredient (more sugar than lime), and the signature green is two artificial dyes; yellow 5 carries a warning label across Europe about children's attention. The least like a lime of anything on the list.

REAL Santa Cruz Organic Pure Lime Juice

The easy accessible pick. Entire ingredient list is two words: organic lime juice. Not from concentrate, no sugar, no preservatives, no dye. USDA organic and bottled to taste like an actual lime. Two tablespoons equals about one fresh lime; found in most grocery stores for a few dollars.

REAL Lakewood Organic Pure Lime Juice

From a Florida family juice company that presses fruit. Organic and fresh pressed rather than boiled to concentrate and reconstituted, so it keeps more of the fruit's real character. Ingredient list is lime juice. no sweeteners, no dye, no benzoate. Found at Whole Foods and natural grocers, usually in a tall glass bottle; costs a bit more because pressing real fruit costs more than reconstituting powder.

REAL Italian Volcano Lime Juice

The best. tastes closest to staying in a grove. Organic Sicilian limes grown in volcanic soil around Sicily, hand harvested and pressed right away, nothing added: no preservatives, no sugar, no concentrate. Real Vinegar

How to read this aisle: Real vinegar starts as something you can name. wine, apples, malted barley, rice. and ferments twice until it's sour. The words that tell you you're being shorted are caramel color, added sugar, flavor, and the strange one, 'non-brewed.' Read the ingredient list and look for a source: real vinegar names what it's made from and keeps the list short. If you see caramel color, added sugar, flavor, or the words non-brewed, put it back.

SKIP White balsamic vinegar

The gourmet-looking pale bottle that sounds like a sophisticated cousin of regular balsamic, but is usually a plain industrial white wine vinegar with sugar and coloring added, cooked light so it stays golden. No decades-long barrel aging like real balsamic. a young, sweetened vinegar styled to look refined, charging gourmet prices for the costume.

SKIP Cheap red wine vinegar

The bargain bottles are frequently rushed through fermentation in steel tanks in days, then propped up with caramel coloring to deepen the red and sulfates to stabilize it, instead of being matured in wood for a year or two. If a red wine vinegar needs added caramel to look red, the wine wasn't doing the work. a thin, harsh, colored-up imitation.

SKIP Flavored and infused vinegars (raspberry, fig, garlic)

A true raspberry vinegar is made by fermenting raspberry wine; the supermarket version is usually a cheap vinegar base with added sugar, natural and artificial flavor, and a splash of red dye to fake the fruit. Ordinary vinegar with candy flavoring stirred in, not fruit turned to vinegar. a flavored sugar vinegar in a farmer's-market disguise.

SKIP Sherry vinegar and champagne vinegar (cheap bottles)

The names promise a specific place and long aging. Real sherry vinegar comes from Jerez, Spain aged years in barrels; real champagne vinegar from that French wine region. But because those names aren't always protected on a US shelf, plenty of bottles are ordinary white wine vinegar with a little flavoring and a fancy label sold at a premium. you can pay triple for a name that means almost nothing about what's inside.

SKIP Light/diluted vinegars and preserved pickling bottles

The watered-down one. 'Lite' and salad vinegars are cut with extra water down to a weaker acidity, so you're paying for a bottle that's part water by design. Some pickling and bulk vinegars add preservatives like sodium benzoate or sulfites and a shot of caramel color. extras that have no business in something supposed to be two ingredients. Real vinegar doesn't need preservatives; it IS a preservative.

SKIP Non-brewed condiment / imitation vinegar

The worst. never fermented anything at all. Makers start with synthetic acetic acid (historically even distilled from wood), then add water, caramel coloring for the brown, and a little citric acid for bite. No wine, no malt, no apples, no fermentation, no mother. In Britain the law won't even let them call it vinegar. it legally has to say 'non-brewed condiment.' A flavored chemical wearing vinegar's clothes.

REAL Pompeian red wine vinegar

The accessible everyday hero; a brand doing Mediterranean staples since 1906. A real wine vinegar's ingredient list is wine vinegar. that's it. No caramel coloring and no added sugar, made by fermenting actual wine until it's tart; the color comes from the wine, not a dye bottle.

REAL Marukan rice vinegar (genuine brewed, unseasoned)

Clean, traditionally brewed from a Japanese maker brewing vinegar for centuries. Real rice vinegar is rice and water fermented into alcohol then vinegar; Marukan's genuine brewed version has no added sugar, salt or MSG. Get the plain unseasoned bottle, not the sweetened sushi version (which adds sugar and salt to fake it).

REAL Sarson's malt vinegar

The best. genuine brewed malt vinegar, brewed in Britain since 1794, the way chip-shop vinegar is supposed to be. Made by malting barley, brewing it into an ale, and letting that ferment into vinegar and age, which is why it has that rich, toasty grain flavor a synthetic non-brewed condiment can never touch. Malt vinegar that actually came from malt.

Clean Baking Powder

How to read this aisle: Baking powder needs only three things: baking soda, an acid to react with it, and a little starch to keep it dry. A clean acid is something like cream of tartar or monocalcium phosphate; the aluminum acid to avoid is sodium aluminum sulfate. Turn the can over and look for the words 'aluminum-free' on the front; if you see sodium aluminum sulfate in the ingredients, put it back. You can make your own aluminum-free powder by mixing cream of tartar with baking soda plus a pinch of cornstarch.

SKIP Store brands like Walmart's Great Value Double Acting

The cheapest can; built on sodium aluminum sulfate, the same aluminum acid as the name brands, only with a generic label and a lower price. You're not avoiding anything by going cheap. you're getting the identical aluminum for less when the aluminum-free can sits 2 feet away for nearly the same money.

SKIP KC Baking Powder

An old-school heritage name, but the label has sodium aluminum sulfate right alongside the baking soda and cornstarch. The vintage can and heritage feel don't change what's inside. the standard aluminum double-acting formula in a nostalgic costume.

SKIP Davis Baking Powder

A kitchen staple many recipes call for by name; ingredient list is cornstarch, baking soda (sodium bicarbonate), sodium aluminum sulfate, monocalcium phosphate. There's the aluminum again doing the same job it does in every conventional can. People buy Davis because the recipe said Davis, never turning the can over.

SKIP Clabber Girl

The most trusted red can in America; the regular double-acting version is built on sodium aluminum sulfate. The kicker: the same company that makes Clabber Girl also owns and makes Rumford, a completely aluminum-free baking powder. they know how to make it without the metal but sell the aluminum version under the more famous label because it's cheaper to produce.

SKIP Calumet (owned by Kraft Heinz)

The worst. the classic that practically defined the metallic baking-powder taste, around since the 1880s. Ingredient list: sodium aluminum sulfate AND calcium sulfate, a double dose. Around 2012 Europe tightened its rules on aluminum-based food additives; here it's still the default acid.

REAL Store-brand aluminum-free powders (Whole Foods 365, Trader Joe's)

The easy budget pick; some store brands do it right. The 365 aluminum-free can's whole list is cornstarch, monocalcium phosphate and baking soda. no aluminum, clean acid, and a price that barely differs from conventional. Read the front for 'aluminum-free.'

REAL Bakewell Cream (Bangor, Maine)

A WWII-era substitute invented by Maine inventor Byron Smith when baking powder and cream of tartar were scarce. Built on sodium pyrophosphate and cornstarch with no aluminum at all; combined with baking soda it makes very tender biscuits. A specialty item mostly found online or in the Northeast.

REAL Hain Featherweight

The pick for anyone watching their heart. not only aluminum-free but sodium-free too. Ingredients: monocalcium phosphate, potato starch, and potassium bicarbonate instead of sodium bicarbonate, so it leavens without adding sodium. Found in the baking or health aisle and online.

REAL Bob's Red Mill

The accessible all-rounder; an aluminum-free double-acting powder. sodium acid pyrophosphate, baking soda, cornstarch and monocalcium phosphate. so you get the delayed two-stage rise without the metal, plus non-GMO and gluten-free, built to skip the metallic aftertaste. In nearly every grocery store.

REAL Rumford

The best and original gold standard. Invented in the 1850s by Harvard chemistry professor Eben Horsford, the first aluminum-free baking powder in America. More than 160 years later still only cornstarch, baking soda and monocalcium phosphate. no aluminum ever. In practically every supermarket, usually right next to the aluminum cans for about the same price.

Real Jam

How to read this aisle: Flip the jar over and read the first three ingredients. if a sweetener shows up before the fruit, you're spreading candy and calling it breakfast. Don't trust the orchard on the label or the cursive on the lid. The honest jar is almost always sitting right next to the dishonest one for the same few dollars.

SKIP Smucker's strawberry jam

Right after strawberries the ingredient list hits high fructose corn syrup, then corn syrup, then sugar. three sweeteners stacked back-to-back before any second fruit. About 50 calories and 12 g sugar per tablespoon, nine of them added. A strawberry-scented sugar delivery system wearing a gingham apron, surviving on nostalgia.

SKIP Welch's Concord grape jelly

The Concord grape flavor is genuinely good but it's riding on high fructose corn syrup to get there. Soft, slick set; about 50 calories and 10 g sugar per tablespoon, almost all added. Banking on muscle memory and the jar your mom bought. the real grape flavor gives the corn syrup a free pass it hasn't earned.

SKIP Kraft strawberry jam

The very first ingredient isn't fruit. it's sugar; strawberries come second. More added sugar than actual berry. About 50 calories and 12 g sugar per tablespoon. It skips the high fructose corn syrup (a small mercy) but leading with plain sugar before the fruit isn't the win the label implies. Sweetened red paste with a strawberry rumor, coasting on brand trust.

SKIP Great Value grape jelly (Walmart)

A giant jar for around $2. Sweetness comes from corn syrup AND high fructose corn syrup, both near the top before the grape juice gets a word in. Wobbly, overly firm translucent purple; one-note grape candy without the tang of real Concord grape. About 50 calories and 9 g added sugar per tablespoon. The 'honest' one in a strange way. cheap, tastes cheap, doesn't pretend otherwise.

SKIP Sam's Choice Strawberry Rhubarb (Walmart)

'Preserves' should mean whole or large fruit pieces bringing some fiber, but this manages 0 g dietary fiber. Thick jammy sweetness where the rhubarb is more a marketing word than a taste; loaded with sugar and the famous rhubarb tartness buried. Reviewers call it generic and cloying. Paying a premium (three-four dollars) for a fancier label on the same sugar. a costume change, not an upgrade.

SKIP Smucker's Sugar Free

Marketed as taste without the guilt but the sweetness comes from sucralose (Splenda) standing in for the sugar they pulled out, with a slightly chemical aftertaste. Texture turns oddly slick and dense. Lower in calories, but trading corn syrup for an artificial sweetener isn't the clean swap the label implies, and you're still not getting much actual fruit. At around four dollars, diet theater aimed at your anxiety.

REAL Polaner All Fruit

The easiest upgrade, at a regular grocery store. Sweetness comes from fruit juice, not corn syrup and not added white sugar; real fruit leads the ingredient list. Brighter, more berry-like, less like candy. A tablespoon runs lighter on added sugar (around 8 g) than almost everything in the skip pile, and it costs about the same as the corn syrup jars. A seedless version wins over picky kids.

REAL Crofter's Organic

Started in Ontario in the late 1980s. organic fruit, low heat, and not much else. Certified organic, packed with real fruit, about a third less sugar than a standard preserve, no high fructose corn syrup and nothing artificial. Thick and fruit-heavy, closer to crushed berries than jelly; rich and a little tart. Around four or five dollars. above budget jars but paying for fruit content instead of filler.

REAL Bonne Maman

The best; started in France in 1971. Ingredient list is short enough to read aloud: fruit, sugar, cane sugar, a little concentrated lemon juice, and fruit pectin. No high fructose corn syrup, no artificial color, no preservatives. Whole pieces of fruit, soft generous texture. NOT a low-sugar jar. it uses genuine sugar and plenty of it. but every sweet gram sits next to real fruit instead of corn syrup, and at four or five dollars it costs about what the dishonest jars cost.

Real Cereal

How to read this aisle: Federal law requires ingredients listed in order of weight, but there's no rule requiring different types of added sugar to be combined into one line. so companies split sugar into three or four differently named sweeteners so no single one climbs high enough on the list to catch your eye. The one place sugar-stacking can't hide is the 'added sugars' line on the nutrition facts panel. a single combined number the FDA has required since 2016. Count every different sweetener name in the ingredient list; if you count three or more scattered through it, check the added-sugars line before it goes in your cart. Divide the added-sugars grams by four to get teaspoons and measure that many into a bowl next to your cereal to see it (12 g = three teaspoons).

SKIP Post Honey Bunches of Oats

Turn the box around and you'll find sugar, brown sugar, corn syrup and honey listed as separate entries spaced out across the ingredient panel rather than grouped where a shopper would notice. Four separately named sweeteners do the same job a single larger sugar entry would, but none looks alarming individually. Splitting the sweeteners also flattens perceived sweetness so it's easier to pour a bigger bowl.

SKIP Malt-O-Meal Oat Blenders with Honey

Leans on '11 vitamins and minerals' and a wholesome oat-cluster look, but carries four separate sweeteners too. sugar, corn syrup, molasses and honey. an even wider spread across the panel than Honey Bunches of Oats. The vitamin messaging does the talking on the front while the four-sweetener sugar spread gets none.

SKIP Kellogg's Frosted Mini-Wheats

Covered in '48 g of whole grain,' 'excellent source of fiber' and '10 layers of wheat' messaging. All true, but doing visual work to keep your eyes off 12 g of added sugar per serving (roughly a third of a day's limit). Short ingredient list. whole grain wheat, sugar, brown rice syrup, gelatin. but the fiber and whole-grain claims are in big bold type while the sugar count sits quietly on the side panel.

SKIP Post Great Grains Raisins, Dates and Pecans

Marketed 'less processed' toward an older, discerning shopper, but carries three separately listed sweeteners. sugar, brown sugar and corn syrup. adding up to 13 g sugar per serving, more than several supposedly less-mature cereals on the same shelf. The dates and raisins already provide natural sugar, making the three added sweeteners even less necessary.

SKIP General Mills Honey Nut Cheerios

The single best-selling cereal in the US. Lists sugar, honey and brown sugar syrup as three separate sweeteners spaced apart on the panel. An independent ingredients report says it carries roughly 12 times the added sugar of plain Cheerios in the same serving, with some comparisons finding it carries more added sugar than Fruit Loops. yet the box still carries the cholesterol-lowering heart-healthy claim from the original unsweetened version.

SKIP Kashi

The worst on the list. not a sugar-stacking story but a settled lawsuit. Built its identity on 'all natural' and 'nothing artificial' on the box for years. A 2011 California class action alleged those claims were false since the products contained synthetic and hexane-processed ingredients (pyridoxine hydrochloride, calcium pantothenate and other lab-produced additives). In 2014 Kellogg settled for $5 million and agreed to drop both claims from packaging nationwide.

REAL Familia (Appenzell, Switzerland)

Founded 1959, developed the first commercially produced Bircher muesli. 'No added sugar' line: whole grain rolled oats, whole grain wheat flakes, fruit flakes, raisins, millet flakes, roasted hazelnuts, wheat germ, roasted almonds. every bit of sweetness from real fruit, none added, none hiding behind a second or third name. Found at natural grocers and increasingly mainstream supermarkets, generally five to six dollars.

REAL Bob's Red Mill (Milwaukie, Oregon)

Founded 1978; mills whole grains the slow stone-ground way and is now employee-owned (Bob Moore began transferring it to employees in 2010, completed 2020). Hot cereals and muesli built from ingredient lists made almost entirely of whole grains, no sweetener-stacking. Found at natural grocers and a growing number of mainstream stores, generally four to six dollars.

REAL Nature's Path Organic (British Columbia)

The best. founded 1985 by Arjan and Ratana Stephens; became the first certified organic breakfast cereal manufacturer in North America, refusing GMO ingredients and synthetic pesticides. The ingredient list matches what the front of the box promises, sweetener for sweetener. Found at natural grocers and most mainstream supermarkets, generally the same price range as the skip brands.

Real Butter Cookies

How to read this aisle: A real cookie is flour, butter, sugar, eggs, maybe chocolate or vanilla. the butter is the whole point. When cookies bake and brown they form acrylamide (a probable carcinogen); Europe legally requires bakers to reduce it, in the US it's only a voluntary suggestion. Turn the package over and look for the fat: if it says butter you're holding a real cookie; if it says palm oil, soybean oil, or vegetable oil, it's a costume. Real butter has a real name. everything else is oil that learned to smell like a bakery.

SKIP Chips Ahoy (Nabisco)

The fat doing the work isn't butter. it's a mix of canola and palm oil propped up with natural and artificial flavor. For decades made with partially hydrogenated (trans-fat) oil until the FDA ban with a 2018 deadline; palm oil replaced it. No butter in there, and there never really was. a cookie this simple shouldn't need a lab to taste like a cookie.

SKIP Oreo (Nabisco)

Never promised butter, but built entirely on oil: palm and canola oil, sugar, and natural and artificial flavor. Like Chips Ahoy, spent decades on partially hydrogenated trans fat before the 2018 ban pushed it to palm oil. A sandwich of oil-and-sugar wafers around an oil-and-sugar filling engineered for shelf life and dunkability. you're paying premium snack money for some of the cheapest fats in the building.

SKIP Keebler (owned by Ferrero)

Slogan 'uncommonly good' hides the usual story: vegetable oils instead of butter, a leavening agent called sodium acid pyrophosphate, and in some varieties like the fudge stripes high fructose corn syrup. Refined flour, cheap oil, sugar and additives. the same formula as half the aisle. No butter carrying the flavor; a promise the ingredient list doesn't keep.

SKIP Famous Amos (owned by Ferrero)

A heartbreaker. started in 1975 as the real thing (Wally Amos's premium fresh-baked butter cookie) but Wally lost control by 1985 and the brand was cheapened through four owners into a mass-produced palm oil version. The butter's gone, the freshness is gone. a hard little palm oil cookie trading on a name its founder didn't even own anymore.

SKIP Lorna Doone (Nabisco shortbread)

The worst. gives the whole game away. Real shortbread is butter, flour, sugar and a pinch of salt, with butter as the star. Lorna Doone's list has soybean oil, canola oil AND palm oil. three cheap oils standing in for the one fat the cookie is built on. plus high fructose corn syrup and artificial flavor. A shortbread with no butter in it at all.

REAL Pepperidge Farm butter cookies (like the Chessmen)

Company started in 1937 by Margaret Rudkin, who baked to feed her son real high-quality food. Pick up a bag of Chessmen and you'll find butter right there in the ingredients, the way a butter cookie should be made. Note: not every Pepperidge Farm cookie is made with butter, so check the bag. but the butter cookies are the real deal, in every grocery store and affordable.

REAL Tate's Bake Shop

Founder Kathleen King started baking at age 11 at her family's Long Island farm stand; discovered more butter and longer baking made a thin crispy cookie. Ingredient list reads like a home baker's kitchen: chocolate chips, flour, butter, cane sugar, eggs, baking soda, salt, vanilla. Real butter, no high fructose corn syrup, no palm oil. In the green-and-white bag at most grocers; costs more because butter costs more than oil.

REAL Walker's Shortbread (Scotland)

The best. Joseph Walker opened a village bakery in Speyside in 1898 to bake the world's finest shortbread; still family-run. Made with exactly four ingredients: flour, pure creamery butter, sugar and salt. No oils, no corn syrup, no artificial flavor, no preservatives. Butter is the second ingredient and the entire soul of the cookie. the opposite of the Lorna Doone. In the red tartan box at nearly every grocery store.

Real Chips

How to read this aisle: A real potato chip needs three things: a whole potato, a stable cooking fat, and salt. 'Natural flavor' can hide more than a hundred separate compounds under FDA rules. the most abused phrase in the grocery store. The napkin test: press one chip firmly into a clean white napkin for 30 seconds. a chip fried in stable fat (avocado, coconut, lard) leaves a small light dry mark; a chip fried in refined seed oils leaves a wide yellow translucent grease ring that spreads like a stain. Then smell the napkin: fresh fat smells like nothing; old oxidized seed oil smells faintly metallic, like crayons, like the inside of an old gym bag. Watch for the 'and/or oil' clause. it means the oil isn't a recipe choice, it's whichever was cheapest.

SKIP Pringles

The first ingredient is not potato. it's dried potatoes (flakes), followed by vegetable oil (a blend of corn, cottonseed, soybean and sunflower depending on supply), then wheat starch, then rice flour, then maltodextrin. By weight closer to a cracker than a chip, which is why they stack perfectly (real sliced potatoes don't stack). Pringles even fought a UK legal battle arguing they shouldn't be classified as potato chips for tax purposes and lost.

SKIP Lay's Classic

Marketed 'made with three ingredients: potatoes, oil, salt,' but the fine print says the oil is 'sunflower, corn, and/or canola oil.' That 'and/or' means the oil changes depending on which was cheapest the week it was fried. you're buying a commodity hedge, not a recipe. Parent PepsiCo has been named in multiple consumer class actions over 'natural' and 'no preservatives' labeling.

SKIP Doritos Nacho Cheese

Ingredient list runs more than 30 items. whey, MSG, disodium phosphate, disodium inosinate, disodium guanylate, artificial color including yellow 6, yellow 5 and red 40. The 'cheddar cheese' is technically present the way a drop of lemon juice in a swimming pool makes lemonade; the flavor is engineered in a lab. The orange dust is a coating system designed to trigger a craving response.

SKIP Cheetos Crunchy

Enriched corn meal, vegetable oil, whey, cheddar cheese, canola oil, maltodextrin, salt, whey protein concentrate, MSG, artificial color and the same disodium twins as Doritos. The orange color is yellow 6, a petroleum-derived dye banned or warning-labeled in several European countries. Frito-Lay reformulates to sell it without the dye in the UK but not in the US.

SKIP Ruffles Cheddar and Sour Cream

The bag says cheddar and sour cream, but cheddar cheese appears after maltodextrin, after salt, after whey, after natural and artificial flavors, and after MSG. Since ingredients are listed by descending weight, the actual cheddar weighs less than the lab-built flavor system designed to taste like it. you're paying premium shelf price for a flavor simulation.

REAL Jackson's

The realest. started by a mother who rebuilt her seizure-disorder son's diet around clean fats. Ingredient list is three items: sweet potato, avocado oil, sea salt. No natural flavors, no maltodextrin, no and/or oil clause. They publish their oil source and their farms. Roughly four to five dollars a bag.

REAL Boulder Canyon Avocado Oil Kettle

One of the first mainstream brands to switch entirely to avocado oil on its core line. Three ingredients: potatoes, avocado oil, sea salt. Kettle cooked (slower frying at lower temperatures = less oil oxidation per chip). Found in most regular grocery stores for around $3.50. the value play of the category and the video's top single recommendation to put in your cart.

REAL Siete

Started by a Texas family as a workaround for a daughter's autoimmune condition. Built on cassava flour and avocado oil, with no seed oils, no dairy, no gluten and no artificial flavors; the sea salt variety is just cassava flour, avocado oil, sea salt. Sourcing and certifications are public. Acquired by a larger company in 2024 (formulation unchanged as of filming, so watch the label going forward).

REAL Late July Organic Sea Salt

The most overlooked. Classic organic tortilla chips: organic stone-ground white corn, organic sunflower or safflower oil, and sea salt. Sunflower and safflower are still seed oils (a fair criticism), but the cold-pressed organic versions are nowhere near the refined industrial oils in mainstream brands, and the rest of the label is genuinely clean. no flavor systems, no dyes, no disodium anything. Price within a dollar of the mainstream brand two shelves away.

Real Mineral Water

How to read this aisle: Real mineral water comes from an underground source protected by law, bottled right at the spring, carrying naturally occurring minerals (calcium, magnesium, bicarbonate) with nothing added or taken out. In Europe 'natural mineral water' is legally protected; in the US 'sparkling water' can mean almost anything, including tap water with bubbles. Read the label: if it says 'carbonated water and natural flavors' that's tap water with bubbles; if it lists sucralose it's a soda. Real mineral water names its source and lists the minerals that came with it. everything else is fizz and marketing.

SKIP LaCroix

Label is just 'carbonated water and natural flavors'. no minerals, no spring, no source; purified water with bubbles and a flavor essence dressed up as premium. A 2018 class action accused its maker of falsely calling it all-natural (heavily disputed, company denied it). Undisputed: in the 2020 Consumer Reports test it came back at 1.16 parts per trillion of PFAS, over the 1 ppt line many scientists consider the safe ceiling.

SKIP Bubly (PepsiCo)

Same story, bigger machine. Carbonated water, natural flavor, nothing from the ground. In the 2020 Consumer Reports study it tested at 2.24 parts per trillion of PFAS. higher than LaCroix and more than double the 1 ppt safety line. A soft-drink company's flavored seltzer, not mineral water.

SKIP Sparkling Ice

Closer to a diet soda than a spring water. After carbonated water: sucralose (an artificial sweetener ~600x sweeter than sugar), plus potassium benzoate (preservative), citric acid, added colors, and a sprinkle of vitamins to sound wholesome. Sucralose raises questions about gut bacteria, and with a pH around 3 it's acidic enough to wear at tooth enamel like soda. To its credit it was clean on the PFAS test, but it's a sweetened, preserved, colored beverage wearing the word water as a costume.

SKIP Polar

The name tells the truth. at least it's seltzer, meaning carbonated purified municipal tap water, not mineral water from a source. But in the 2020 Consumer Reports study Polar's Natural Seltzer came back at 6.41 parts per trillion of PFAS, the second highest of every sparkling water checked, more than six times the recommended ceiling. Two strikes: never mineral water to begin with, and one of the heaviest forever-chemical loads in the test.

SKIP Topo Chico

The worst. a cult favorite that does come from a real source in Mexico and says 'natural mineral water' on the label (Coca-Cola paid $220 million for US rights in 2017). But in the 2020 Consumer Reports test it had the highest PFAS level of every product tested, 9.76 parts per trillion, nearly 10 times the safe ceiling. After the report Coca-Cola upgraded filtration and by 2021 the level dropped to about 3.9. better but still above the 1 ppt line.

REAL Mountain Valley Spring Water

The American one. pharmacist Peter Green and his brother John began bottling in 1871 from a protected spring in the Ouachita Mountains outside Hot Springs, Arkansas, one of the oldest bottled waters in the country. One protected source, bottled at the spring, the same water for over 150 years; poured at the White House and US Senate. Lighter in minerals than the European bottles but the real thing (there's a sparkling version in heavy glass bottles). A few dollars a bottle.

REAL Gerolsteiner (Germany)

Bottled since 1888 from springs in the volcanic Eifel region; comes out of the ground already sparkling, naturally carbonated from the source. Around 2,500 mg of minerals per liter including a big dose of calcium and magnesium. actual nutrition, not flavoring. The best-selling natural sparkling mineral water in Europe; around $4 a bottle at better grocers.

REAL San Pellegrino

The best. flowing from springs at San Pellegrino Terme in the Italian Alps foothills since 1899; the water takes about 30 years filtering through mountain rock before bottling. Ingredient list says one thing: carbonated mineral water, with calcium and magnesium naturally there, no sweeteners, colors or flavor essence. In the same 2020 Consumer Reports test it came back at 0.31 parts per trillion of PFAS, well under the safety line. Around 5 to 6 dollars for the big green bottle.

Real Beef Jerky

How to read this aisle: Real jerky is a lean cut of beef sliced thin, seasoned with salt and spice, and dried. meat and time. The two things to hunt for on the back of the bag are added sugar and sodium nitrate. Watch for the celery-powder loophole: 'cultured celery extract' / celery juice powder cures meat with vegetable-sourced nitrate so a brand can claim 'no nitrite' while still curing with it. The WHO classifies processed (cured, nitrate-treated) meat as a group-one carcinogen with sufficient evidence it raises colorectal cancer risk. Turn the bag over and read the first few ingredients: if you see beef, salt and spices it's real jerky; if you see sugar near the top, cultured celery extract, sodium nitrate, or mechanically separated chicken, put it back.

SKIP Oberto

The one wearing the health halo. around since 1918, leans hard on 'all natural,' which tells you nothing about sugar or salt. Oberto's original beef jerky carries around 600 mg of sodium in a single ounce (roughly a quarter of a day's limit) plus added sugar. The mildest skip and the beef itself is real, but a snack can be all natural and still be a salt-and-sugar bomb, and this leans that way.

SKIP Jack Link's

America's #1 jerky since 1986. Front brags 'no added MSG' and 'no nitrites,' but the back has brown sugar near the top, then hydrolyzed corn protein and yeast extract (a source of free glutamate, the active part of MSG by another name), then cultured celery extract. the loophole, since celery powder is naturally loaded with nitrate that turns into nitrite during processing. So they print 'no nitrite' while curing the meat with nitrate from a vegetable. A clean label, not a clean product.

SKIP Krave

Jerky that thinks it's dessert. started 2009, bought by Hershey (a candy company) in 2015. On the garlic chili jerky the second ingredient right after beef is evaporated cane syrup (sugar); a single small serving carries around 8 g, candy territory not steak territory. Skips synthetic nitrite but uses celery juice powder (its own dodge). When the second thing in your jerky is syrup, you're eating meat-flavored candy at a protein-snack price.

SKIP Old Trapper

At least honest on the label. around since 1969, spells sodium nitrate right out at the end (no celery-powder costume), plus brown sugar as the second ingredient and hydrolyzed corn protein for savory punch. Curing with nitrate that meets heat and digestion can form nitrosamines, which are carcinogenic. the reason processed meat carries the WHO group-one classification. Europe is forcing nitrate levels down; US limits are looser. Honest about being cured, sweetened, processed meat, but honesty doesn't make nitrate and sugar a health food.

SKIP Slim Jim (Conagra)

The worst. people think of it as a beef stick but it is not. The second ingredient right after 'little beef' is mechanically separated chicken: a paste forced off the carcass under pressure until it comes out like a puree. Conagra mixes that with corn syrup, soy protein, sodium nitrate and sodium phosphate and presses it into a stick. Shares a shelf with beef jerky but is a fundamentally different, far more processed product. chicken paste and corn syrup in a tube.

REAL Chomps

Started 2012 by Pete Maldonado (a personal trainer) and Rashid Ali who wanted a clean grab-and-go protein. Meat sticks. same format as a Slim Jim but made of real beef: 100% grass-fed and finished beef, no added sugar, and every stick hand-inspected. The honest version of a meat stick. Found at most grocery stores and online.

REAL Country Archer (now called Archer)

Origin from a 2011 roadside stand near the Grand Canyon where founders tasted jerky by artisan butcher Charlie Merriweather (making it since 1977) and bought his San Bernardino business. 100% grass-fed beef, zero sugar, no hormones, no antibiotics, no preservatives. called 'the Sam Adams of beef jerky,' whole muscle beef and real seasoning. Found at places like Sprouts and Wegmans; costs more because grass-fed beef costs more.

REAL People's Choice Beef Jerky (Los Angeles)

The best and oldest. John Bianchetti opened a downtown LA butcher shop in 1929 and his family has handcrafted jerky there ever since, four generations. The old-fashioned jerky is thick-cut whole-muscle beef, slow-dried the same way for nearly 100 years, and the label is almost empty: no sugar, no MSG, no nitrites, no preservatives. just beef and real spices from beef raised on US soil. Chewy and rugged the way jerky used to be. Found online and in some grocery stores.

Read Any Label in 10 Seconds

The whole method in five moves. This is everything the 170 verdicts in this guide have in common.

1. Skip the front entirely. The front of the package is an ad. Words like natural, artisan, farmhouse, premium and heritage are marketing terms with no legal weight. Every verdict in this guide came from the back panel.
2. Read the first three ingredients. They are listed by weight, so the first three ARE the product. If the first ingredient of a "wheat" bread is enriched white flour, you have your answer in three seconds.
3. Count the list. Real versions of most staples need 2 to 6 ingredients: butter is cream and salt, cottage cheese is milk, cream and salt, jerky is beef, salt and spices. When a staple runs 15 or 30 items, the extras are doing a job the real ingredient should be doing.
4. Know the five cover words. Natural flavors (a flavor lab product), yeast extract and hydrolyzed protein (MSG under other names), cellulose (wood pulp filler), seasoning solution (injected water), and juice or syrup concentrates (sugar). One of these near the top of a list is usually the whole story.
5. Let price per pound break ties. Some of the cleanest picks in this guide are store brands (Kirkland, Aldi Countryside Creamery, Whole Foods 365) and some of the worst offenders are the most expensive names on the shelf. Price signals margin, not quality. Read the panel, then pay the lowest price for the clean list.
