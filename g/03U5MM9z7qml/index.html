Real Cottage Cheese

How to read this aisle: The tilt-and-drain test: tip the tub on its side. real cottage cheese releases a thin, watery whey that runs clear and fast; a gummed-up tub's liquid is thick, gluey and slow, clinging to the curd in ropes. The three-ingredient test (the one that never lies): read only the first three ingredients. You want cultured milk, cream and salt. If you see starch, maltodextrin, a gum, or a word you can't pronounce in those first three lines, put it back. The protein trick: if a tub screams a huge protein number on the front but lists whey protein concentrate high in the ingredients, that protein was boosted with powder, not built from real milk. Real curd doesn't need powder to be high in protein.

SKIP Breakstone's

Heritage brand (founded 1882) but tapioca starch comes right after milk and cream, doing the job cream should do; sodium runs high. The flavored 'cottage doubles' cups fall apart completely, stacking modified corn starch, added sugar, natural flavor and potassium sorbate (a preservative); under 12g protein. 'a dessert in a health costume.'

SKIP Corto

Breakstone's West Coast twin, same parent company (selling the heritage story since 1919). Low-fat tub lists modified food starch, calcium phosphate, xanthan gum and guar gum, plus added whey to prop up the protein number; sodium climbs to around 400mg per half-cup serving.

SKIP Hood

One of the most heavily engineered tubs found anywhere. A single low-fat container can carry whey protein concentrate, modified food starch and three separate gums (guar, locust bean and carrageenan), along with potassium sorbate and phosphoric acid; whipped and flavored versions pile on more. 'When a label needs three different gums just to hold itself together, the milk underneath was never rich enough.'

SKIP Great Value (Walmart store brand)

Cheap isn't the crime. what makes it cheap is. Stabilizer blend reads like a supplement bottle: maltodextrin, guar gum, carrageenan, mono- and diglycerides, and locust bean gum, finished with potassium sorbate. Fat-free version is busier and some tubs add polysorbate 80. The gums do the cream's job for pennies.

SKIP Prairie Farms

Markets hard on farm families, blue ribbons and World Dairy Expo awards, but the ingredient list has a stabilizer blend of five separate thickeners. guar gum, mono- and diglycerides, xanthan gum, carrageenan and carob bean gum. plus maltodextrin and potassium sorbate. 'The trophy on the front does not match the lab work in the back.'

SKIP Country Fresh (and regional value tubs following the same recipe)

Cultured nonfat milk dried out then rebuilt with maltodextrin, guar gum, carrageenan, mono- and diglycerides and locust bean gum, with natural and artificial flavor and a preservative. 'When the first real ingredient is fat-free milk and the next six lines are thickeners, you are buying engineering with a dairy logo on it.' Regional tubs rotate names region to region, so only the label is a reliable defense.

REAL Daisy

Entire ingredient list is three words. cultured skim milk, cream, salt. No starch, no gums, no preservatives. Delivers about 13g protein and usually costs about $3.50, often the cheapest tub on the shelf. 'The most honest cottage cheese in the store is also one of the cheapest.'

REAL Good Culture

Label openly promises no gums, no carrageenan, no preservatives, and the ingredients back it up: milk, cream, sea salt, and live and active cultures. About $5.60, but you're paying for real dairy and probiotics; about 14g protein. Founder built it after he couldn't find an additive-free cottage cheese; the brand chefs and recipe developers reach for.

REAL Friendship Dairies

Making cottage cheese for more than 100 years without ruining it: cultured nonfat milk, milk, salt and enzymes. no gums, no starch. Some tubs reach 16g protein the old-fashioned way with real dairy, and it's usually cheaper than the boutique options.

Real Mozzarella

How to read this aisle: The two words that tell you you're being shorted are 'cellulose' and 'imitation.' The honest move on shredded cheese isn't a pre-shredded bag. buy the block and shred it yourself (avoids the anti-caking coating and the higher contamination risk of pre-cut cheese). Read the ingredient list, not the big print. a '100%' or 'real cheese' claim on the front doesn't override added wood fiber and starch on the back. SKIP Great Value Shredded Mozzarella (Walmart store brand)

After the cheese itself, lists potato starch and powdered cellulose (the polite name for wood pulp) added to prevent caking, plus natamycin (a mold-inhibiting fungicide). It was Walmart's Great Value grated cheese caught in the 2016 lab testing with cellulose levels far past what the industry considers acceptable. The coating is why store-brand shreds turn greasy and clumpy when melted.

SKIP Sargento

Markets itself as '100% real natural cheese,' and the cheese is real, but the shredded mozzarella still carries potato starch and natamycin coating every shred. the same anti-caking treatment as the cheaper bags. In 2024 Sargento recalled a batch of shredded cheese over possible listeria contamination (that batch went to food service, not branded bags), a reminder that pre-shredded, surface-treated cheese carries more risk than a sealed block.

SKIP Kraft

Shredded mozzarella carries the same lineup. potato starch, cellulose powder, calcium sulfate and natamycin. Earned its spot because of the receipt: in 2016 Kraft Heinz was named in class-action lawsuits over its '100% grated parmesan,' which independent testing pegged at around 3.8% cellulose. Kraft's defense was that the wood pulp was within legal limits. 'which is true and also exactly the problem.' The '100%' claim can't be backed up by the ingredients.

SKIP String cheese (national brands or store versions)

Low-moisture part-skim mozzarella that's been heated and pulled into a peelable rope then individually wrapped. It started as real mozzarella, but by the time it's processed for peel, shelf life and a kid's backpack, it's a snack product more than a cheese. often part-skim and rubbery, built to survive a lunch box rather than to taste like much.

SKIP Imitation mozzarella (on cheap frozen pizzas)

Often labeled 'imitation mozzarella' right on the box. Contains water, vegetable oil, potato and modified starch, a milk protein called rennet casein, gelatin, and titanium dioxide (a white pigment added to make oil and starch look like cheese). Not cheese at all. vegetable oil, starch and a chemical whitener engineered to stretch and melt like the real thing.

Real Shredded Cheese

How to read this aisle: The water-dissolve test: drop a handful of shredded cheese into warm water and stir 30 seconds. Real cheese softens, clumps together and releases visible milk fat; filler-heavy cheese leaves a cloudy, starchy residue and the shreds resist breaking down because the cellulose/starch coating acts as a waterproof barrier. Milky-white water with grit at the bottom is the filler separating, not cheese dissolving. The bag-shake test: shake the bag hard for 5 seconds and look at the bottom. A light dusting of powder is normal; a thick, visible layer of white powder means heavy cellulose and starch coating you're paying for by weight. Filler names to watch for: cellulose (powdered wood pulp), potato starch, corn starch, natamycin (synthetic mold inhibitor). The same filler playbook runs across every price point from budget to premium. only the marketing changes.

SKIP Kraft

Bags say 'natural cheese' on the front in bold, but the ingredient list includes cellulose powder, potato starch, corn starch, calcium sulfate and natamycin. a triple filler system plus a synthetic mold inhibitor on a product marketed as natural. Kraft's parent has faced class-action scrutiny over the word 'natural' on products containing processed wood fiber and synthetic preservatives.

SKIP Great Value (Walmart store brand)

Ingredient list includes cellulose, potato starch, corn starch and natamycin. Priced as the budget option, but the filler-to-cheese ratio means less real cheese per ounce than the label suggests. The thick layer of white powder at the bottom of the bag is the anti-caking cocktail you're paying for by weight.

SKIP Borden

Heritage dairy name (over 160 years) but heritage doesn't equal quality. contains cellulose, potato starch and natamycin, the same industrial filler recipe as every budget brand. Borden filed for bankruptcy in 2020 citing declining milk prices and crushing debt; under that financial pressure every product line gets optimized for margin.

SKIP Crystal Farms

Markets itself as mid-tier with packaging implying farm freshness; ingredient list includes potato starch, powdered cellulose and natamycin. Home melt tests reveal a gritty, separated texture. a hallmark of excessive starch coating; the shreds don't melt smoothly because they're cheese wrapped in a layer of industrial powder.

SKIP Kroger brand

Private-label ingredient list includes cellulose, potato starch, corn starch and natamycin. the exact same filler profile as Kraft and Great Value, repackaged under a store brand that implies freshness and local sourcing. 'There is nothing local about powdered cellulose.'

SKIP Sargento

Built its entire brand identity around being real, natural cheese and charges a premium for it, but flip the bag: potato starch, powdered cellulose, natamycin. the exact same three fillers found in the budget brands it markets against. 'Arguably the widest marketing-to-reality gap on the entire shelf.' You're paying for a brand story, not a better product.

REAL Tillamook

Farmer-owned cooperative (founded 1909, Oregon coast). Uses real aged cheese with only potato starch as an anti-caking agent. no cellulose, no corn starch, no natamycin. Remarkably short ingredient list; cooperative ownership means farmers control the product. About $1–$2 more per bag than Kraft. for a product that is actually what it claims to be, 'that is not a premium, that is a bargain.'

REAL Cabot

Cooperative of New England dairy farm families (founded 1919). Shredded cheese contains potato starch only for anti-caking in most varieties. no cellulose, no natamycin, no corn starch. Sits in the same price range as Sargento but with an ingredient list that actually delivers on the promise Sargento only markets.

REAL Organic Valley

Cooperative of organic farmers (founded 1988). USDA certified organic, organic cheese as the primary ingredient, only potato starch for anti-caking. no cellulose, no natamycin, no corn starch, no modified food starch, no natural flavors. 'The cleanest shredded cheese widely available.' Most expensive (about $2–$3 more per bag than Kraft) but you're paying for cheese, not wood pulp and starch. the gold standard on the shelf.

Where To Buy Butter

How to read this aisle: Butter has a legal definition (21 USC 321a, 21 CFR 101.67): to be called butter it must contain at least 80% milk fat. that's the entire bar. There's no separate tier for supermarket vs premium vs gourmet, so once a product clears 80% it's legally the same product regardless of price or packaging. The price you pay has almost nothing to do with what's in the package and almost everything to do with which door you walked through. Before your next run, check your regular store's per-pound butter price against Aldi's. if the gap is more than a dollar or two, you're paying for the name on the door, not quality. The three recommended stores all sell grade AA butter well above the 80% milk-fat floor from cows never given rBST, for roughly $3–$4/lb consistently without games.

SKIP Walmart (Great Value salted butter)

Mildest offender. cheapest on sticker price (about $2.42 for an 8-oz pack, under $5/lb) and not overcharging you. But in a structured side-by-side taste test against other store brands, testers flagged it specifically for being oversalted to the point the salt overwhelmed the actual dairy flavor. Still earns grade AA, but 'acceptable range' isn't 'well-balanced.' You get exactly what you paid for and not a cent more in quality.

SKIP Winn-Dixie

A straight receipt: a multi-reporter survey priced an identical Thanksgiving-staples basket at 19 stores; Walmart rang it up at $76.65, Winn-Dixie at $105.67. a 38% markup for the exact same items, same week, same market, with butter one of the line items driving the gap. Not marketed as a discount chain, but shoppers who treat it as a normal-priced everyday store are paying real money basket after basket.

SKIP Publix

Highest basket total of the three compared ($109.62, even above Winn-Dixie). A separate area survey found Publix charging $5.26–$5.79/lb for standard sweet cream butter versus $3.69/lb at Aldi in the same market. roughly 40–50% more for a legally identical product. Its GreenWise organic line is genuinely well-reviewed and competes honestly at $9.59/lb, but the everyday plain sweet-cream stick most people grab carries the 40–50% premium with nothing on the label to justify it.

SKIP Whole Foods ('whole paycheck')

A documented, repeatable pricing pattern. In the same multi-store comparison, a dozen large eggs cost $1.39 at Target but $4.19 at Whole Foods. three times the price, same product category, same week and region. That gap shows up as a consistent pattern across the whole dairy case, butter included. When the markup is this consistent across basic staples, 'premium sourcing starts to look less like a principled stance and more like a pricing strategy with a values-based paint job.'

SKIP The Fresh Market

The biggest number on the list: charged $9.59 for a single pound of plain butter. the highest recorded anywhere in the comparison. Full 18-item basket rang up at $74.82 versus $37.55 for the identical items at Aldi. essentially double. For a plain stick of butter with no organic certification, no imported origin story, nothing distinguishing it on the label, that $9.59 tag is 'the single most expensive way to buy a legally identical product anywhere in this lineup.'

REAL Aldi (Countryside Creamery)

Aldi's in-house butter, $3.23–$4.09/lb depending on week and location, dropping as low as $1.99/lb during Thanksgiving/Christmas seasonal sales. undercuts nearly every other store. Grade AA, cows certified rBST-free. In blind side-by-side comparisons against Kerrygold (which costs 2–3x more), plenty of tasters genuinely couldn't tell the Irish-style Countryside Creamery apart from the real thing. Butter freezes for up to a year, so stocking up on the $1.99 sale can cut annual butter spending by half or more.

REAL Costco (Kirkland Signature)

Sells in 4-lb bulk packs for around $11.79–$12.99, which works out to roughly $2.95–$3.25/lb. in line with everyday prices, sometimes cheaper. Same grade AA standard, same legal butter classification, just sold at scale with warehouse-club margins. The only catch is committing to 4 lbs at once, which freezer storage solves. 'One of the best per-pound deals in the entire grocery landscape.'

REAL Trader Joe's

$3.99/lb. not the single cheapest (Aldi undercuts it) but offers consistency: no weekly sales to chase, no markup games. Grade A cream from cows never given rBST hormone, priced the same every time whether a random Tuesday or the week before a holiday. Reviewers call it solid, dependable, supermarket-quality butter; also stocks well-regarded imported options (a French cultured butter, sometimes a New Zealand import) for the premium tier without premium-grocer prices.
